About

I am a recent graduate of the University of Minnesota where I studied geographic information science and computer science. I graduated in May 2018 with a Masters of Geographic Information Science degree and an M.S. minor in computer science. Before my Masters, I graduated from the University of Minnesota with a Bachelor of Science in geography and a Bachelor of Arts in music. My interest in GIS and spatial computing revolves around my deep interest in its development and application to studying the earth system and earth/environmental science data. I'm particularly interested in GIS and spatial computing and their use in climatology, remote sensing, and ecology. Most recently I've become interested in machine learning and deep learning and their application to satellite images and spatial data.

I currently work as a post-graduate intern in the Advanced Short-Term Research Opportunity program at Oak Ridge National Laboratory. Here, I work in the Geographic Information Science and Technology group assisting with the development of high resolution settlement and neighborhood maps from satellite imagery using machine learning and image processing techniques.

Spatial Operations in Deep Learning for Satellite Image Classification

Abstract
Convolutional neural networks (CNNs) are the current state-of-the-art method used for image classification tasks. They have been shown to achieve impressive results on a variety of well-known computer vision datasets and in some cases have been able to outperform humans at image recognition tasks. However, deep learning and classification of satellite images, or more broadly geospatial data, has not yet been explored to the same extent as classification of everyday objects that are typically found in images taken by handheld cameras. Moreover, current methods do not take full advantage of the spatial information contained in geospatial data. A CNN builds a representation of the input features and their spatial properties through its convolutional and pooling layers, however, this spatial awareness is limited and not clearly understood. Here, I explore how methods from spatial statistics can be incorporated into CNN layers and the image preprocessing step to increase classification accuracy on satellite images. Specifically, I examine how measures of spatial autocorrelation can be used to better exploit the spatial properties inherent in satellite images. These ideas are tested using the satellite image dataset Functional Map of the World. Results from this research will provide insights into how CNNs understand spatial processes and whether incorporating spatial statistics into CNNs or the preprocessing step can improve classification accuracy.

DROught MOnitering Toolbox (DROMOT)

The DRought MOnitering Toolbox is a set of tools written in Python for processing spatiotemporal data. Its original purpose was for calculating the Keetch-Byram Drought Index and McArthur's Forest Fire Danger Index but it has since grown to include functionality outside the realm of drought indices. It currently supports both vector and raster spatiotemporal data models and a variety of methods related to remote sensing and climate summary statistics. In the future I plan to make this project into a Python package for easier use and distribution.
